# Literature Review

## 1. Cyber Dost

### Introduction
* **Overview:** Cyber Dost (meaning "Cyber Friend") is an official cybersecurity and cyber-safety awareness initiative launched by the Indian Cybercrime Coordination Centre (I4C), under the Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA), Government of India.
* **Purpose:** It aims to educate citizens on safe internet practices, digital hygiene, and preventive measures against online frauds.

### Features
* **Awareness Campaigns:** Actively disseminates tips, warnings, and guidelines about evolving cyber threats (such as digital arrests, UPI scams, and fake travel bookings) across print and digital media.
* **Citizen Safety:** Provides an approachable framework to help vulnerable groups, particularly women and children, understand and avoid cybercrimes.
* **Volunteer Program:** Allows citizens to register as "Cyber Volunteers" to flag unlawful online content or become promoters of cyber awareness.
* **Essential Guidance:** Directs victims to vital resources like the 1930 Cyber Crime Helpline and the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal.

### Strengths
* **High Credibility:** Being an official government initiative, it provides highly trusted and verified safety information.
* **Proactive Outreach:** Maintains an active social media presence (e.g., the official Cyber Dost X/Twitter handle) to keep the public updated on the latest scam trends.
* **Community Engagement:** Effectively crowdsources threat detection by allowing ordinary citizens to participate through the volunteer program.

### Limitations
* **Lack of Interactive Applications:** It primarily relies on one-way communication like social media posts and static campaigns, rather than a dedicated, interactive mobile application.
* **No Real-Time, Step-by-Step Guidance:** While it directs users to helplines, it lacks an integrated conversational AI chatbot or regional-language voice assistant to calm and guide a panicking victim during a live scam event.

## 2. National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (1930)

### Introduction
* **Overview:** The National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (cybercrime.gov.in) is the central government platform managed by the Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA). 
* **Purpose:** It is designed to facilitate the reporting of all types of cybercrimes by citizens, with a dedicated 1930 toll-free helpline for immediate financial fraud reporting.

### Features
* **Categorized Reporting Modules:** The portal divides complaints into distinct categories for organized processing:
  * Women/Children Related Crime (with options to register anonymously or register and track).
  * Financial Fraud.
  * Other Cyber Crimes.
* **Learning Corner:** A dedicated section for public education that includes:
  * **Citizen Manual:** A document explaining the portal's functionalities and workflow.
  * **Cyber Safety Tips:** Guidelines to protect data and devices.
  * **Cyber Awareness & Daily Digest:** Educational content about emerging threats and cyber fraud modus operandi.
* **"Report Suspect" Facility:** A feature allowing citizens to report suspicious URLs, WhatsApp numbers, social media handles, or SMS headers.

### Strengths
* **Structured Complaint Routing:** By categorizing crimes (especially separating women/children related crimes with an anonymous option), the system ensures complaints reach the appropriate law enforcement departments quickly.
* **Centralized Resources:** It acts as a single, highly credible repository for both reporting crimes and accessing official educational manuals.
* **Tracking Capabilities:** Users can register and actively track the status of their complaints, providing transparency in the investigation process.

### Limitations (The Gap for Rural Users)
* **High Cognitive Load:** The interface is highly text-dense and formal. A rural user who just lost money in a UPI scam might not immediately know if they should click "Financial Fraud" or "Other Cyber Crime," causing confusion during a panic situation.
* **Language and Literacy Barriers:** The platform relies heavily on reading comprehension. While there may be a language toggle, the reliance on dense text manuals ("Citizen Manual", "Daily Digest") makes it inaccessible for users with low digital literacy.
* **Lack of Step-by-Step Interactive Guidance:** The portal expects the user to read the manual and figure out the reporting workflow themselves. It completely lacks a conversational AI or voice assistant to hold the victim's hand and verbally guide them step-by-step through the complaint registration process.

## 3. Digital Literacy Programs

### PMGDISHA (Pradhan Mantri Gramin Digital Saksharta Abhiyan)

* **Introduction:** PMGDISHA is a flagship initiative under the Digital India program managed by the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY). Its primary objective is to bridge the digital divide by making at least one person in every eligible rural household digitally literate.
* **Features:** * **Structured Curriculum:** Offers a 20-hour training course (spread over 10 to 30 days) delivered in official Indian languages.
  * **Core Modules:** Teaches citizens how to operate digital devices (smartphones, tablets), browse the internet, send emails, and access e-governance services (like PAN and banking).
  * **Digital Payments:** Includes specific training on executing cashless transactions using UPI, USSD, and mobile banking.
  * **Execution:** Implemented free of cost at the grassroots level via Common Service Centres (CSCs).
* **Strengths:** * **Massive Grassroots Reach:** Successfully crossed its target of training over 6 crore rural individuals.
  * **Inclusive Empowerment:** Specifically targets marginalized sections of society, including BPL families, women, minorities, and differently-abled persons.
  * **Standardized Evaluation:** Ensures quality through independent external evaluation by national agencies like NIELIT and IGNOU.
* **Limitations (The Gap):** * **Static Curriculum:** The 20-hour course focuses heavily on basic operational skills. It lacks continuous, dynamic updates regarding rapidly evolving, real-time cyber threats (like fake parcel scams or AI voice cloning).
  * **No Ongoing Emergency Support:** Once the training is completed, users do not have a dedicated, interactive platform (like an AI Chatbot) to consult if they are actively targeted by a new UPI or OTP scam.

### ASEAN Digital Literacy Programme (ASEAN DLP)

* **Introduction:** The ASEAN DLP is a flagship initiative by the ASEAN Foundation, supported by Google.org. Its primary focus is to combat misinformation and disinformation across underprivileged communities in Southeast Asia.
* **Features:** * **Train-the-Trainer Model:** Trained 1,000 educators and youth leaders who subsequently empowered over 100,000 individuals in their local communities.
  * **Multilingual Platform:** Launched DigitalClassASEAN.org, an e-learning platform to help users recognize and counter misinformation.
  * **Targeted Research:** Conducted regional studies on how underprivileged communities consume and respond to online information.
* **Strengths:** * **Focus on Critical Thinking:** Goes beyond basic device operation to teach media literacy, cultural understanding, and digital responsibility.
  * **Youth Mobilization:** Successfully engaged over 700,000 people online through youth-led movements for digital resilience.
* **Limitations (The Gap):** * **Geographical Focus:** It is designed for ASEAN member states and does not directly address the localized, rural Indian context.
  * **Focus on Misinformation over Financial Fraud:** The program heavily emphasizes combating fake news rather than providing direct, step-by-step remediation for financial cybercrimes like UPI and banking frauds. 
  * **Lack of Real-Time Assistance:** It lacks an automated, voice-enabled assistant to help users immediately verify a suspicious link or message in real-time.

## 4. Existing Cyber Awareness Applications

### Introduction
* **Overview:** A variety of mobile applications are available on digital storefronts like the Google Play Store, ranging from government-backed initiatives to independent educational tools. Notable examples include *Cyber Hygiene by ISEA* (Information Security Education and Awareness under MeitY), *ICSAS by NIELIT*, and independent quiz applications.
* **Purpose:** These mobile applications are designed to provide on-the-go security tips, host interactive quizzes, and deliver bite-sized educational materials directly to citizens' smartphones.

### Features
* **Gamified Quizzes and Assessments:** Applications like the *Cyber Security Awareness Quiz* utilize multiple-choice questions (MCQs) to test user knowledge on password hygiene, malware defense, and phishing red flags.
* **Official Media Repositories:** Platforms like *ICSAS by NIELIT* host digital multi-media materials, including short video lectures and standard "Do’s and Don'ts" visual graphics.
* **Localized Handbooks:** The *Cyber Hygiene app by ISEA* provides downloadable, localized safety manuals, handbooks, and standard internet security guidelines directly curated by the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY).

### Strengths
* **High Portability:** Mobile applications allow users to access cybersecurity education at any time without needing to navigate complex web portals.
* **Active Learning via Gamification:** The inclusion of mini-games and scoring systems keeps users engaged far better than static web pages.
* **Authoritative Directives:** Government-backed apps provide credible, verified instructions that help citizens establish standard device configuration baselines.

### Limitations (The Gap)
* **Severe Language Constraints:** Despite some applications offering translated static PDFs or handbooks, the core interactive components and user interfaces remain predominantly available only in English or Hindi. They lack deep regional language integration (such as fluid Telugu, Tamil, or Kannada interfaces) for rural demographics.
* **Absence of Intelligent Conversational Interfaces:** The applications rely entirely on rigid menu buttons, videos, and pre-scripted text forms. There are zero instances of a conversational AI chatbot (like a localized Gemini-powered assistant) to interpret natural human queries.
* **No Voice-Driven Assistance:** The existing applications offer no voice input or spoken output layers. For users with low textual literacy in semi-urban and rural areas, navigating these applications to find specific scam mitigation techniques remains functionally impossible.

## 6. Research Gap 

### The Gap
While the existing digital safety infrastructure provides a robust foundation for national cybercrime management and basic digital literacy, a critical accessibility gap remains at the community level. The current systems expect users to adapt to complex web forms, static text-heavy manuals, and formal institutional jargon. 

This presents a profound operational mismatch for semi-urban and rural populations targeted by localized UPI and OTP frauds. These users often lack the reading comprehension or technical confidence to navigate dense government portals during a highly stressful financial scam event. They require intuitive, frictionless, and immediate intervention.

### The CyberRakshak Solution
To transform passive awareness into active community defense, a localized, adaptive solution is required. The proposed **CyberRakshak** platform directly bridges the identified research gaps by introducing:
* **A Gemini-Powered Conversational AI Chatbot:** Capable of understanding and interpreting everyday, natural descriptions of digital frauds rather than relying on strict categories.
* **Multi-Lingual Voice Support:** Allowing low-literacy users to speak naturally in regional languages (e.g., Telugu, Hindi, Tamil) and receive spoken instructions, completely bypassing the text barrier.
* **Empathetic, Low-Friction Step-by-Step Guidance:** Breaking down complex legal remediation steps (like freezing a bank account or finding a UTR number) into highly simplified visual and vocal cues during panic situations.
